# AirWise
## Pre-Seed Investor Memo

Compliance and monitoring software for covered buildings

April 2026

## 1. Executive Summary

AirWise is a software-first compliance and monitoring platform for owners and operators of covered buildings, starting in New York City. The company helps customers consolidate building data, assess Local Law 97 exposure, maintain audit-ready records, and layer in monitoring data where live operational visibility improves compliance and planning.

The near-term wedge is not a full building-performance operating system. It is a narrower and more credible workflow:

- unify fragmented building, document, and utility data
- estimate building-level compliance exposure and reporting readiness
- identify missing inputs and operational risk
- provide monitoring-backed visibility where it improves decision-making
- deliver portfolio-level dashboards and building scorecards for owners, engineers, and asset managers

NYC is the initial beachhead because LL97 is already active, the covered-building base is large, and owners are under pressure to make portfolio decisions with incomplete data and expensive incumbent tooling. Boston and Chicago are logical follow-on markets because they face similar emissions and disclosure pressure, but the company should validate the model in NYC first.

AirWise is currently at the `local pilot MVP and pilot-hardening` stage. The repo now includes a working local product across portfolio onboarding, LL97 compliance, filing-package preparation, documents, monitoring, recommendations, and supervised command workflows. The next company objective is to turn that working product surface into a tighter pilot deployment motion and the first repeatable paid portfolio.

## 2. Problem

Covered-building owners face a compliance and operating-data problem, not just a reporting problem.

In New York City, LL97 has already forced owners to understand building-level emissions exposure, data quality, and retrofit timing. Most owners do not have a single system that can answer basic portfolio questions with confidence:

- Which buildings are covered, at risk, or likely compliant?
- What data is missing or unreliable?
- Which buildings need engineering review first?
- How should monitoring or retrofit work be prioritized?
- What documentation will be needed for internal planning and external reporting?

Today, these answers are typically spread across:

- benchmarking records
- utility bills and Portfolio Manager exports
- engineering reports and capital plans
- spreadsheets managed by consultants or asset managers
- siloed controls and monitoring systems

The result is slow decision-making, inconsistent reporting readiness, and poor visibility at the portfolio level.

The pain is especially acute in affordable and regulated housing, where owners face real budget constraints and often cannot absorb rising compliance and operating costs easily. But the problem is broader than affordable housing. It affects NYC covered-building owners generally, especially portfolios with multiple properties and limited internal systems for compliance operations.

## 3. Why Now

Three conditions make this an attractive starting point now.

### 3.1 LL97 has created an immediate workflow problem

For covered buildings in NYC, compliance is no longer theoretical. Owners need a repeatable way to assess emissions exposure, organize source documents, and track what is known versus assumed. This pressure creates a willingness to adopt software that reduces uncertainty and coordination cost.

### 3.2 Existing workflows are fragmented and expensive

Many owners rely on a mix of consultants, spreadsheets, benchmarking exports, and point solutions. Legacy vendors can be expensive and often do not provide a complete compliance workflow tied to portfolio planning and document traceability.

### 3.3 Comparable regulations are expanding

Boston and Chicago are not the first target markets, but they matter strategically. Both represent nearby expansion opportunities once the NYC product and GTM model are validated. This supports a broader category story: software for covered-building compliance operations, beginning with NYC.

## 4. Product

AirWise should be understood as a three-layer product.

### 4.1 System of record

The system of record unifies the information needed to understand a building or portfolio:

- building profile and identifiers
- uploaded owner documents
- utility and benchmarking records
- portfolio metadata
- assumptions, notes, and source attribution

This gives the customer a single working environment instead of disconnected files and point tools.

### 4.2 Compliance engine

The compliance layer turns the underlying data into actionable outputs:

- covered-building status and building profile
- emissions and exposure estimates based on available inputs
- reporting-readiness and missing-data flags
- building-level scorecards
- portfolio ranking by risk, uncertainty, and likely next action
- audit trail showing what came from public data, what came from the owner, and what remains unverified

This is the core product value at the pre-seed stage.

### 4.3 Monitoring layer

The monitoring layer adds live visibility where it improves compliance planning or building operations:

- device and sensor integrations
- interval or near-real-time operational data where available
- anomaly visibility
- better evidence for prioritizing engineering review or intervention

Monitoring is part of the wedge, but it should be framed as visibility and decision support first, not as a fully automated controls product on day one.

### 4.4 Pilot workflow

The initial customer workflow is straightforward:

1. ingest building list and available owner files
2. enrich each building with public data
3. assess compliance exposure and reporting readiness
4. identify missing data and uncertainty
5. recommend monitoring or engineering next steps where needed
6. deliver building scorecards and a portfolio dashboard

That is enough to create customer value before advanced automation is proven.

### 4.5 What is true today vs. what must be proven

**Already true in the current product**

- owners need a consolidated workflow for LL97-era compliance operations
- NYC public data and owner-provided data can be combined into a practical portfolio baseline
- monitoring can improve visibility where existing records are incomplete or stale
- operators are dissatisfied with fragmented and expensive workflows
- AirWise already supports a local MVP workflow across compliance, filing, documents, monitoring, and supervised command review

**Must be proven through pilot execution**

- how much time the workflow saves versus current practice
- how well pilots convert into annual software contracts
- how much monitoring improves prioritization and customer retention
- which adjacent modules customers will pay for after the core compliance workflow is in place

**Longer-term roadmap, not current proof**

- controls automation
- advanced optimization across HVAC or water systems
- predictive maintenance at scale
- insurer-facing data products

## 5. Customer and Beachhead

### 5.1 Initial customer profile

The initial target customer is a New York City owner or operator with a multi-building covered portfolio and a clear need for compliance visibility. This includes:

- affordable housing operators
- mixed-income and multifamily portfolios
- owners with limited internal compliance tooling
- operators currently stitching together consultants, spreadsheets, and incumbent software

Affordable housing remains an attractive entry segment because compliance pressure is acute and budgets are constrained, but the company should not position itself as limited to that segment.

### 5.2 Beachhead strategy

The initial beachhead is NYC covered buildings. This keeps the company focused on one regulatory context, one primary buyer profile, and one data environment while the product and deployment motion are still being proven.

Boston and Chicago should be described as follow-on markets after NYC validation, not as simultaneous execution priorities.

### 5.3 Current traction posture

AirWise should describe current traction as `working local MVP`, `pilot discussions`, and `pilot hardening`, unless there is a signed deployment commitment. That framing is more accurate than describing the company as pre-product.

## 6. Go-To-Market

The go-to-market should start narrow and relationship-led.

### 6.1 Phase 1: pilot hardening and first deployment

The first objective is to convert an interested operator into a pilot on a small number of buildings. The pilot should produce:

- building-level scorecards
- a portfolio dashboard
- a clear data-quality and missing-inputs view
- a recommended next-step plan for monitoring or engineering review

The purpose of the pilot is to prove workflow value and create the basis for an annual portfolio contract.

### 6.2 Phase 2: portfolio expansion

Once the first pilot is successful, expand to a full portfolio contract for the same customer. This is the most efficient path to early ARR because it uses an existing buyer, existing data work, and proven urgency.

### 6.3 Phase 3: warm-network replication

Use the initial customer and domain network to reach similar NYC owners and operators. The early GTM should rely on:

- founder-led sales
- owner and developer relationships
- engineering and implementation credibility
- a product demo grounded in real building workflows

Only after this motion is working should the company treat inside sales or city expansion as a priority.

## 7. Business Model

The commercial model should be kept simple at this stage.

### 7.1 Pricing structure

The current pricing should be presented as an initial hypothesis, not a finalized enterprise pricing architecture:

- pilot / onboarding fee
- annual software subscription at the building or portfolio level
- optional monitoring and integration services

This is more credible than presenting several polished long-term tiers before the first repeatable deployment is proven.

### 7.2 What customers are paying for

Customers are paying for:

- compliance workflow clarity
- portfolio visibility
- reduced manual coordination
- better prioritization of next actions
- a system of record that can support owners, engineers, and compliance stakeholders

The business case should not depend on claiming fully validated energy savings before pilot data exists.

### 7.3 Growth logic

Near-term growth should be described through milestones, not precision forecasting:

- secure signed pilot
- convert first pilot into a paid portfolio relationship
- make deployment repeatable across similar owners
- win second and third portfolio customers

That sequence is more believable than detailed five-year precision at this stage.

## 8. Why This Team

The technical founder is responsible for product, engineering, infrastructure, AI-assisted workflows, and the overall system design required to ship a software-first platform quickly.

The domain and GTM counterpart brings direct industry context, buyer access, and practical knowledge of how owners evaluate building technology and compliance tooling.

Together, this creates an early-stage advantage:

## 10. Risks and Mitigations

### 10.1 Product scope risk

**Risk:** trying to build compliance software, controls automation, predictive maintenance, and a hardware business at once.

**Mitigation:** keep the wedge focused on compliance and monitoring; defer advanced automation until the core workflow is proven.

### 10.2 Proof risk

**Risk:** making savings and operational claims before AirWise-specific data exists.

**Mitigation:** frame benchmark-based savings as modeled opportunity and use the pilot to validate what is real.

### 10.3 Deployment risk

**Risk:** on-site integrations vary across buildings and can slow early delivery.

**Mitigation:** make the pilot valuable even without deep automation; focus first on data consolidation, scorecards, and monitoring-backed visibility.

### 10.4 Data quality risk

**Risk:** owner records and public data are incomplete, inconsistent, or difficult to reconcile.

**Mitigation:** make missing-data detection and confidence scoring part of the product, not a hidden operational problem.

### 10.5 GTM risk

**Risk:** relationship-led early sales do not translate into repeatable growth.

**Mitigation:** use early customers to define a repeatable onboarding motion and only broaden GTM after the first portfolio conversions are working.

## 11. Appendix

### Appendix A: Market framing

The market should be framed as a sequence, not a giant unsupported top-down number:

- NYC covered buildings as the beachhead
- affordable housing and other multi-building operators as strong early customer segments
- adjacent regulatory cities such as Boston and Chicago after NYC validation

### Appendix B: Pilot scope

A practical initial pilot can cover `3-5` buildings and should deliver:

- portfolio overview
- building scorecards
- missing-data map
- monitoring recommendations
- next-step action memo

### Appendix C: Benchmarks and modeled opportunity

Any savings, capex, or operational-impact assumptions included in investor or customer discussions should be labeled clearly as:

- industry benchmark
- modeled opportunity
- subject to pilot validation

Competitor outcomes and public research may be useful context, but they should not be presented as if they are already AirWise results.
